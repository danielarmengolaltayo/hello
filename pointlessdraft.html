Pointless, 2021 (draft)

We live in times dominated by a new medium that demands new forms of interaction and communication. A medium that imposes such accelerated rhythms that it hardly leaves us room to breathe, digest or reflect. It seems, that in this scenario, absurdity has become the maxim that moves us, and that only those who take it to the extreme, attracting the greatest number of looks and ears to their discourses, will have the opportunity to influence the behavior of the social body.
For 24 hours straight, I subjected my full attention to a digital bombardment.

Because, if attention is a limited resource that can be exploited, where exactly is that limit?

The bombardment was an experiment specifically designed to test the limits of my state of vigilance and concentration for a long period of time. My only task was to focus my attention on capturing on paper one random instruction per second, automatically generated by a computer, in a sort of guided meditation, stopping as few times as possible, for the shortest possible time.

Once we know that there is a whole market, hidden to plain sight, trading with and fighting for our eyeballs, our clicks or our likes, one might ask, is it possible to regain some agency and control over the flows of our own attention?

Instructions for the performer

1) Sit in front of the paper, the computer and the black and red markers. Focus your attention on the first square delimited by the grid on the paper and start the computer's process (instructions below).

2) If the computer says "yes", paint the square you are focused on, using the black marker and move to the next square (left to right, top to bottom) until you reach the end of the paper.

3) If you lose your attention, stop the computer's process, paint the next empty square with the red marker and when you are ready to continue, turn on the computer's process again and repeat step 2.


Instructions for the computer

1) Emit a "yes" or a "no", randomly, every second.
Each print has space for 3600 (50 x 72) pixels, equivalent to one order per second, for one hour.
